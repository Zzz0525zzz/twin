# Scripta Materialia Letter-style Introduction Draft v1

Mechanical compression in ferroelectrics is inseparable from domain-mediated strain accommodation. In tetragonal perovskites, symmetry-related variants carry coupled spontaneous polarization and spontaneous strain; uniaxial loading can therefore bias variant stability, drive ferroelastic reorientation and non-180° switching, and redistribute stress through compatibility constraints [1–4]. The resulting response depends not only on lattice elasticity, but also on how domain-wall motion, pinning or clamping, and local stress transfer organize deformation across the domain structure [1–6].

Extensive work has shown that ferroelectric and ferroelastic switching are strongly shaped by boundary conditions, defects, interfaces, grain constraints, and local stress fields [2–10]. Diffraction and time-resolved measurements quantify switching fractions and averaged lattice strains, while real-space imaging resolves nucleation, wall displacement, pinning, and domain patterns retained after loading [3–8,10]. These approaches establish when reorientation begins, how much material transforms, and where walls move. What remains difficult to read from them is how the same external compression is converted into spatially organized strain accommodation when the initial domain architecture is changed. In particular, comparable changes in the polarization component along the loading direction do not necessarily imply comparable stress–strain responses, because compatibility constraints and wall-mediated stress redistribution are folded into the same macroscopic signal.

Here we examine this problem in BaTiO3, a representative tetragonal ferroelectric in which spontaneous polarization, ferroelastic strain, and non-180° reorientation are directly coupled. The 90° DW configuration and the reference configuration are subjected to the same y-axis uniaxial compression, fixing the material system and loading path while changing the initial domain architecture. The comparison reveals markedly different compressive responses despite a partly shared evolution of the loading-axis polarization component. This contrast is traced to how the initial domain architecture organizes ferroelastic reorientation and strain redistribution during loading, identifying spatial accommodation of polarization reorganization as the link between initial domain state and macroscopic mechanical response.
